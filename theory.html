Foundations of Data Depth and Multivariate Structure

Let \( (E) \) be a Banach space, \( (\mathcal{B}) \) its Borel \( (\sigma) \)-algebra, and \( (\mathcal{P}) \) a class of probability distributions on \( (\mathcal{B}) \), viewed as empirical distributions assigning equal mass \( (1/n) \) to \( (n) \) observations. A depth function is a mapping \( (D: E \times \mathcal{P} \to [0,1]) \), denoted \( (D(z\mid P)) \), or \( (D(z\mid X)) \) when \( (X\sim P) \), satisfying:

D1 Distribution invariance: \( (D(z+b\mid X+b)=D(z\mid X)) \) for all \( (b\in E) \).

D2 Linear invariance: \( (D(Az\mid AX)=D(z\mid X)) \) for every bijective linear transformation \( (A:E\to E) \).

D3 Null at infinity: \( (\lim_{|z|\to\infty} D(z\mid X)=0) \).

D4 Monotonicity on rays: if \( (z^*) \) maximizes depth, then for any unit vector \( (r) \), the function \( (\alpha \mapsto D(z^*+\alpha r\mid X)) \) is non-increasing (weak sense).

D5 Upper semicontinuity: upper level sets \( (D_\alpha(X)={z\in E: D(z\mid X)\ge \alpha}) \) are closed for all \( (\alpha) \).

D1 and D2 imply affine invariance. D3 and D4 ensure bounded, starshaped upper level sets with respect to a deepest point \( (z^*) \). D5 is a technical regularity condition. Under D4, if \( (X) \) is centrally symmetric about \( (z^*) \), then \( (D(z^*\mid X)) \) is maximal. Central symmetry means \( (X-z^*) \) and \( (z^*-X) \) have identical distributions. Compared to earlier formulations, these postulates do not require upper semicontinuity to ensure maximality at \( (z^*) \). Replacing D4 by D4con yields quasiconcavity, meaning all \( (D_\alpha(X)) \) are convex for \( (\alpha>0) \), defining convex depth. Convexity implies starshapedness and hence D4. D2 may be weakened to invariance under isometries (orthogonal invariance), or further to scale invariance \( (D(\lambda z\mid \lambda X)=D(z\mid X)) \) for \( (\lambda>0) \).

In practice, depth statistics are typically applied in Euclidean spaces \( (\mathbb{R}^d) \). For non-elliptical distributions, one considers distributions with affine dimension \( (d) \), i.e. convex hull of support equals \( (\mathbb{R}^d) \). A distribution is spherical if invariant under orthogonal transformations, and elliptical if \( (X=a+BY) \) with \( (Y) \) spherical. Elliptical distributions have upper level sets \( (D_\alpha(P)) \) forming ellipsoids determined by scatter matrix \( (BB') \). Any affine invariant depth satisfying D1 and D2 yields equivalent depth contours under ellipticity. If \( (P) \) is elliptical with unimodal radial density, there exists a transformation \( (\varphi) \) mapping it to a spherical distribution; thus any depth satisfying D1 and D2 is invariant under such transformations.

Three principal constructions of multivariate depth are distinguished: based on distances from central points or volumes; based on centrality notions such as decreasing weighted means of order statistics; and based on simplices and halfspaces in \( (\mathbb{R}^d) \). These approaches differ in symmetry reflection, robustness to outliers, and scalability in higher dimensions. Depth contours in applications often exhibit near-equidistant behavior across levels.

Most multivariate depths are convex and affine invariant; some also exhibit spherical invariance. Depths differ in contour shape and in whether they uniquely determine the underlying distribution. A fundamental ordering is induced via dilation order: \( (X \preceq_d Y) \) if \( (E[\varphi(X)] \le E[\varphi(Y)]) \) for all convex \( (\varphi:\mathbb{R}^d\to\mathbb{R}) \), raising the question of concordance between depth-based and dilation-based orderings.

Distance-Based Depth Functions

Let \( (E=\mathbb{R}^d) \). Depth based on distances measures outlyingness via distances from a chosen center.

\((L_2)\)-depth (spatial depth):

Defined by

\[ D^{L^2}(z\mid X)=\bigl(1+\mathbb{E}|z-X|\bigr)^{-1}. \]

For a sample \( (x^1,\dots,x^n) \),

\[ D^{L^2}(z\mid x^1,\dots,x^n)=\left(1+\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n |z-x^i|\right)^{-1}. \]

Range is \( ((0,1]) \) with maximum \( (1) \). Upper level sets are convex. The depth vanishes at infinity (D3) and is maximized at the spatial median \( (z^*=\arg\min_z \mathbb{E}|z-X|) \), which coincides with the symmetry center under central symmetry. It satisfies monotonicity on rays (D4), quasiconcavity (D4con), and upper semicontinuity (D5). It depends continuously on \( (z) \) and converges under mild integrability and weak convergence \( (P_n\to P) \). However, it is not consistent with the dilation order, since dilation increases \( (\mathbb{E}|z-X|) \), hence decreases depth.

It is invariant under rigid motions (D1, D2iso), but not affine invariant. An affine invariant version uses the \( (M) \)-norm \( (|z|*M=\sqrt{z'M^{-1}z}) \) with a positive definite matrix \( (M) \), typically \( (S_X) \) satisfying affine equivariance \( (S*{AX+b}=AS_XA') \):

\[ D(z\mid X)=\bigl(1+\mathbb{E}|z-X|_{S_X}\bigr)^{-1}. \]

Sample versions arise by replacing expectations with empirical averages. Common choices for \( (S_X) \) include the covariance matrix or robust estimators such as MVE or MCD.

Mahalanobis depth:

Let \( (c_X) \) be a location functional and \( (S_X) \) a positive definite scatter matrix satisfying affine equivariance. The generalized Mahalanobis depth is

\[ D^{\text{Mah}}(z\mid X)=\left(1+|z-c_X|*{S_X}^2\right)^{-1}. \]

It satisfies D1 to D5. Upper level sets are ellipsoids

\[ D*\alpha^{\text{Mah}}(X)={z:|z-c_X|_{S_X}^2\le \alpha^{-1}-1}, \]

centered at \( (c_X) \). It is continuous in \( (z) \) and in \( (P) \). With \( (c_X=\mathbb{E}[X]) \), \( (S_X=\Sigma_X) \),

\[ D^{\text{Mah}}(z\mid X)=\left(1+(z-\mathbb{E}[X])'\Sigma_X^{-1}(z-\mathbb{E}[X])\right)^{-1}, \]

and its sample version replaces parameters by empirical estimates. Depth contours depend strongly on the choice of \( (S_X) \) (classical vs robust). Mahalanobis depth is determined by first two moments; it does not uniquely identify distributions beyond elliptical families, though within such families contour sets suffice.

Other depths (formulas and notation only):

Projection depth:

\[ D^{\text{proj}}(z\mid X)=\left(1+\sup_{p\in S^{d-1}} \frac{|\langle p,z\rangle-\operatorname{med}(\langle p,X\rangle)|}{\operatorname{Dmed}(\langle p,X\rangle)}\right)^{-1}, \]

where \( (S^{d-1}) \) is the unit sphere, \( (\langle p,X\rangle) \) denotes projection, \( (\operatorname{med}) \) is the univariate median, and \( (\operatorname{Dmed}) \) is the median absolute deviation.

Oja depth:

\[ D^{\text{Oja}}(z\mid X)=\left(1+\frac{\mathbb{E}\big[\operatorname{vol}_d(\operatorname{co}{z,X_1,\dots,X_d})\big]}{\sqrt{\det \Sigma_X}}\right)^{-1}, \]

where \( (X_1,\dots,X_d) \) are i.i.d. copies of \( (X) \), \( (\operatorname{co}) \) denotes convex hull, \( (\operatorname{vol}_d) \) the \( (d) \)-dimensional volume, and \( (\Sigma_X) \) a scatter matrix.

Depth-Trimmed Regions and Outlyingness

For a probability distribution \( (P) \) and level \( (\alpha \in [0,1]) \), the upper level sets

\[ D_\alpha(P)={z\in E: D(z\mid P)\ge \alpha} \]

form a nested family of sets (larger \( (\alpha) \) gives smaller sets). These are called depth-trimmed central regions.

  • The deepest points are those achieving maximal depth: \[ P_{\max}(P)={z: D(z\mid P)=\sup_{x} D(x\mid P)}. \]
  • By D1 and D2, these regions are affine equivariant (stable under translation and linear transformations).
  • Their shape depends on the underlying distribution \( (P) \) (location, dispersion, geometry).

There is a correspondence with convex set-valued statistics:

Given a family \( ({C_\alpha(P)}*{\alpha\in[0,1]}) \) of convex, closed, bounded sets, define

\[ D(z\mid P)=\sup{\alpha: z\in C*\alpha(P)}. \]

This construction satisfies D1–D5 and D4con, hence yields a convex depth function. Thus, depth functions and central regions are equivalent representations.

A depth function induces a center-outward ordering of points: higher depth implies more central.

Define the associated outlyingness function:

\[ \text{Out}(z\mid X)=\frac{1}{D(z\mid X)}-1. \]

Key properties:

  • \( (\text{Out}(z\mid X)=0) \) at the center (maximum depth).
  • \( (\text{Out}(z\mid X)\to \infty) \) as \( (|z|\to\infty) \) (since depth \( (\to 0) \)).

Equivalently,

\[ D(z\mid X) = \bigl(1+\text{Out}(z\mid X)\bigr)^{-1}. \]

Interpretation:

  • Points outside the central region \( (D_\alpha) \) satisfy \[ D(z\mid X)<\alpha \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \text{Out}(z\mid X) > \frac{1}{\alpha}-1, \] hence are classified as outliers at level \( (\alpha) \).

Limitations of Spatial Depth and Motivation for Kernelization

A contour plot of the spatial depth \( D(x \mid X) \) illustrates that depth is highest at the center (spatial median) and decreases outward. Thus, low depth corresponds to peripheral or isolated points, motivating the outlier rule:

\( \text{Classify } z \text{ as an outlier if } D(z \mid X) < t \)

for a suitable threshold \( t \in (0,1) \).

Two methodological issues arise:

  • Selection of the threshold.
  • Whether spatial depth reflects the geometric structure of the data cloud.

Behavior under spherical symmetry

For spherically symmetric distributions, depth contours align with the data geometry (approximately concentric and circular), so depth is inversely related to distance from the center. Hence, low depth reliably indicates outlyingness.

Failure under non-elliptical structure

However, for non-elliptical or complex shapes, this relationship breaks down. The failure arises from the definition:

\( D(x \mid X) = 1 - \left\| \mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{x - X}{\|x - X\|} \right] \right\| \)

  • Points located centrally in terms of geometry may have low depth.
  • Points in low-density regions may still have high depth due to directional cancellation.

Thus, spatial depth does not necessarily align with Euclidean distance or probability density.

Geometric inconsistency

  • Depth contours do not follow the intrinsic shape of the data.
  • Central regions may exclude high-density areas.
  • The induced ordering may not reflect true centrality.

Comparison with Mahalanobis depth

\( D^{\mathrm{Mah}}(z \mid X) = \left(1 + (z - \mu)' \Sigma^{-1} (z - \mu) \right)^{-1} \)

  • Produces elliptical contours regardless of data shape.
  • Fails for non-elliptical distributions.
  • Larger values indicate centrality, unlike spatial depth where smaller values indicate outlyingness.

Fundamental trade-off

Spatial depth depends on the sum of unit direction vectors:

  • Emphasizes direction over magnitude
  • Provides robustness to extreme observations
  • Reduces sensitivity to distance-based separation

Thus,

Robustness to extremes \( \Longleftrightarrow \) Loss of geometric sensitivity

Motivation for kernelization

To address these limitations, spatial depth can be modified by incorporating a similarity measure via a positive definite kernel. Let \( \phi \) be the associated feature map.

\( D(x \mid X) = 1 - \left\| \mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{\phi(x) - \phi(X)}{\|\phi(x) - \phi(X)\|} \right] \right\| \)

  • Captures nonlinear structure
  • Incorporates similarity instead of raw distance
  • Retains robustness through normalization

Kernelized Spatial Depth

A positive definite kernel \( (\kappa:\mathbb{R}^n\times \mathbb{R}^n\to \mathbb{R}) \) induces an implicit feature mapping \( (\phi:\mathbb{R}^n\to \mathbb{F}) \) into an inner product space \( (\mathbb{F}) \) such that

\[ \kappa(x,y)=\langle \phi(x),\phi(y)\rangle. \]

For stationary kernels (e.g. Gaussian \( (\kappa(x,y)=\exp(-|x-y|^2/\sigma^2)) \)), \( (\kappa(x,y)) \) acts as a similarity measure.

The kernelized spatial depth (KSD) evaluates spatial depth in the feature space \( (\mathbb{F}) \). Using the identity

\[ |x-y|^2 = x^Tx + y^Ty - 2x^Ty, \]

the spatial depth expression can be rewritten in terms of inner products and then replaced by kernel evaluations. This yields the sample KSD:

\[ D_\kappa(x\mid X) = 1-\frac{1}{|\mathcal{X}(x)|-1} \left( \sum_{y,z\in \mathcal{X}(x)} \frac{\kappa(x,x)+\kappa(y,z)-\kappa(x,y)-\kappa(x,z)} {\delta_\kappa(x,y)\,\delta_\kappa(x,z)} \right)^{1/2}, \]

where

\[ \delta_\kappa(x,y)=\sqrt{\kappa(x,x)+\kappa(y,y)-2\kappa(x,y)}, \]

and terms with \( (x=y) \) or \( (x=z) \) are set to zero (analogous to the spatial sign at zero).

KSD is a spatial depth in the feature space \( (\mathbb{F}) \), but not necessarily a depth in \( (\mathbb{R}^n) \) since the feature-space center may not correspond to any point in the original space. A new “center” in \( (\mathbb{R}^n) \) is defined as the point maximizing KSD; however, monotonicity away from this center is not guaranteed.

The formulation is valid for any positive definite kernel, with emphasis on stationary kernels. Empirically, KSD contours adapt to the intrinsic geometry of the data, unlike standard spatial depth:

  • For non-elliptical distributions (e.g. half-moon, ring-shaped), KSD contours follow the data shape.
  • Depth values at isolated points are small, enabling effective outlier detection.

Example values: depths at candidate outliers are approximately \( (0.2495) \) (half-moon) and \( (0.2651) \) (ring). A threshold around \( (0.25)–(0.27) \) separates outliers from the main data cloud. Determination of the threshold is addressed separately.

KSD-Based Outlier Detection Algorithm

Let \( (\mathcal{X}={x_1,\dots,x_\ell}\subset \mathbb{R}^n) \) be observations, \( (\kappa) \) a kernel, and \( (t) \) a threshold. Data are generated from a mixture \( ((1-\alpha)F_{\text{good}}+\alpha F_{\text{outlier}}) \). The goal is to classify a new point \( (x) \) as an outlier using kernelized spatial depth (KSD). Computational cost per evaluation is \( (O(\ell^2)) \).

Algorithm (KSD-based outlier detection)

  1. Compute kernel matrix: \[ K_{ij}=\kappa(x_i,x_j), \quad i,j=1,\dots,\ell. \]
  2. For input \( (x) \), compute for each \( (x_i) \): \[ \zeta_i=\kappa(x,x_i), \quad \delta_i=\sqrt{\kappa(x,x)+K_{ii}-2\zeta_i}. \] If \( (\delta_i=0) \), set \( (z_i=0) \); else \( (z_i=\delta_i^{-1}) \).
  3. For all pairs \( ((i,j)) \): \[ \tilde{K}_{ij}=\kappa(x,x)+K_{ij}-\zeta_i-\zeta_j. \]
  4. Compute KSD: \[ D_\kappa(x\mid \mathcal{X})=1-\frac{1}{|\mathcal{X}(x)|-1}\sqrt{z^T \tilde{K} z}. \]
  5. Decision rule: \[ x \text{ is an outlier if } D_\kappa(x\mid \mathcal{X}) \le t. \]

Kernel choice and parameter \( (\sigma) \)

For Gaussian kernel \( (\kappa(x,y)=\exp(-|x-y|^2/\sigma^2)) \), parameter \( (\sigma) \) controls locality:

  • \( (\sigma \to \infty) \): KSD converges to spatial depth; all observations contribute equally (unit direction effect).
  • \( (\sigma \to 0) \): KSD becomes constant \( (1-\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}) \) for all \( (x) \), making data non-informative.

Thus, \( (\sigma) \) determines the trade-off between global (large \( (\sigma) \)) and local (small \( (\sigma) \)) structure. Proper choice yields contours aligned with data geometry.

A generalized Gaussian kernel is:

\[ \kappa(x,y)=\exp\big(-(x-y)^T \Sigma^{-1}(x-y)\big), \]

with \( (\Sigma=\mathrm{Diag}(\sigma_1^2,\dots,\sigma_n^2)) \).

Estimation of scale parameters \( (\sigma_k) \)

(per coordinate \( (k) \)):

  1. \( (\sigma_k=\mathrm{mean}_{i,j}|x_{ik}-x_{jk}|) \) (Gini difference).
  2. \( (\sigma_k=\mathrm{median}_{i,j}|x_{ik}-x_{jk}|) \) (robust Gini).
  3. \( (\sigma_k=\mathrm{mean}_i|x_{ik}-\mathrm{mean}_j x_{jk}|) \).
  4. \( (\sigma_k=\mathrm{median}_i|x_{ik}-\mathrm{median}_j x_{jk}|) \) (MAD-type).

These provide dispersion estimates with increasing robustness; median-based versions are less sensitive to outliers.

KSD vs Density-Based Outlier Detection

The comparison distinguishes kernelized spatial depth (KSD) from density-based outlier detection along three dimensions: definition, estimation, and underlying assumptions.

1. Nature of the function

  • KSD is not a density; it measures outlyingness relative to the data cloud.
  • A density function represents likelihood and takes values in \( ([0,\infty)) \), whereas KSD is bounded in \( ([0,1]) \).

2. Difference from kernel density estimation (KDE)

  • A sample KSD is not equivalent to a kernel density estimator.
  • In KDE, the bandwidth parameter (e.g. \( (\sigma) \) in Gaussian kernels) must tend to zero as sample size increases to ensure consistency.
  • In KSD, the scale parameters (diagonal entries of \( (\Sigma) \)) converge to the true dispersion of the data along each dimension and remain strictly positive.
  • KSD can be constructed using nonstationary kernels (e.g. polynomial kernels), unlike KDE which relies on density kernels.

3. Underlying assumptions about outliers

  • Density-based methods assume outliers lie in low-density regions.
  • Depth-based methods define outliers as points far from the central mass, as measured by depth.
  • These notions can diverge: a point may lie in a relatively dense region yet be far from the majority of observations.

Illustrative example

Data are generated from a mixture

\[ F = 0.11\,U_{[0,0.2]} + 0.9\,U_{[2,12]}, \]

so most observations lie in \( ([2,12]) \), while a small fraction lies in \( ([0,0.2]) \).

  • A density-based method (Gaussian KDE with \( (\sigma=0.06) \)) would first classify observations in \( ([2,12]) \) as outliers before detecting those in \( ([0,0.2]) \), since the latter form a concentrated high-density region.
  • A KSD-based method (e.g. \( (\Sigma=\Sigma_3) \)) with threshold \( (0.2632) \) identifies all observations in \( ([0,0.2]) \) as outliers, along with some points at the extreme end of \( ([2,12]) \), reflecting distance from the main mass.

Thus, KSD captures global structure and centrality, whereas density-based approaches prioritize local concentration.

Kernel Functions and RKHS Foundations

A kernel function \( (\kappa(x,z)) \) computes an inner product in an implicit feature space:

\[ \kappa(x,z)=\langle \phi(x),\phi(z)\rangle, \]

where \( (\phi) \) is an embedding into a Hilbert space. Kernel evaluation on a finite set yields a positive semi-definite (PSD) matrix, motivating a characterization independent of explicit feature construction.

Finite positive semi-definite characterization

A function \( (\kappa: X\times X \to \mathbb{R}) \) is a kernel if it is symmetric and for every finite subset \( ({x_1,\dots,x_\ell}\subset X) \), the matrix \( (K_{ij}=\kappa(x_i,x_j)) \) is PSD. This definition does not require \( (X) \) to be a vector space.

Characterisation theorem

If \( (\kappa) \) is continuous (or defined on a finite domain), then

\[ \kappa(x,z)=\langle \phi(x),\phi(z)\rangle \]

for some feature map \( (\phi) \) into a Hilbert space if and only if \( (\kappa) \) is finitely PSD.

Construction of the feature space (RKHS)

Define a function space

\[ \mathcal{F}=\left\{f(\cdot)=\sum_{i=1}^\ell \alpha_i \kappa(x_i,\cdot)\right\}. \]

This space is closed under addition and scalar multiplication. Define the inner product:

\[ \langle f,g\rangle = \sum_{i=1}^\ell \sum_{j=1}^n \alpha_i \beta_j \kappa(x_i,z_j), \]

for \( (f(\cdot)=\sum \alpha_i \kappa(x_i,\cdot)) \), \( (g(\cdot)=\sum \beta_j \kappa(z_j,\cdot)) \).

  • \( (\langle f,f\rangle = \alpha^T K \alpha \ge 0) \) (PSD property ensures validity)
  • The reproducing property holds: \[ \langle f, \kappa(x,\cdot)\rangle = f(x). \]

Thus \( (\mathcal{F}) \) becomes a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) with feature map:

\[ \phi(x)=\kappa(x,\cdot). \]

Mercer’s theorem

For \( (X\subset \mathbb{R}^n) \) compact and \( (\kappa) \) continuous, symmetric, and inducing a positive integral operator:

\[ (T_\kappa f)(\cdot)=\int_X \kappa(\cdot,x)f(x)\,dx, \]

positivity implies the expansion:

\[ \kappa(x,z)=\sum_{j=1}^\infty \phi_j(x)\phi_j(z), \]

with orthonormal \( ({\phi_j}) \). The series converges uniformly and \( (\sum |\phi_j|^2<\infty) \).

Translation-invariant kernels (example)

If \( (\kappa(x,z)=\kappa(x-z)) \), then it admits a Fourier expansion:

\[ \kappa(u)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n \cos(nu), \]

leading to feature maps based on orthogonal trigonometric functions.

Covariance interpretation

A kernel can be expressed as a covariance:

\[ \kappa_q(x,z)=\int f(x)f(z) q(f)\,df, \]

for a distribution \( (q) \) over functions. This induces embedding

\[ \phi(x)=(f(x))_{f\in \mathcal{F}}, \]

with inner product weighted by \( (q) \).

Closure properties (kernel construction)

If \( (\kappa_1,\kappa_2) \) are kernels, then so are:

  • \( (\kappa_1+\kappa_2) \)
  • \( (a\kappa_1), (a>0) \)
  • \( (\kappa_1 \kappa_2) \) (Schur product)
  • \( (f(x)f(z)) \)
  • \( (\kappa_3(\phi(x),\phi(z))) \)
  • \( (x^T B z), (B\succeq 0) \)

These preserve PSD via matrix operations.

Derived kernels

If \( (\kappa_1) \) is a kernel and \( (p) \) a polynomial with positive coefficients:

\[ \kappa(x,z)=p(\kappa_1(x,z)), \quad \exp(\kappa_1(x,z)), \quad \exp(-|x-z|^2/(2\sigma^2)) \]

are kernels. The last is the Gaussian (RBF) kernel.

Feature space structure

  • Sum of kernels corresponds to concatenation of feature maps
  • Scaling corresponds to rescaling
  • Product corresponds to tensor product features
  • Powers \( (\kappa^s) \) generate features as all monomials of degree \( (s) \)

Gaussian kernel features

The Gaussian kernel corresponds to an infinite-dimensional feature space:

\[ \exp(x)=\sum_{i=0}^\infty \frac{x^i}{i!}, \]

implying all polynomial degrees are present with weights decreasing as \( (1/i!) \).

Wilcoxon Signed Rank Test

Paired Replicates Model and Assumptions

Data consist of paired observations \( (X_i,Y_i) \), \( (i=1,\dots,n) \). Define differences

\[ Z_i = Y_i - X_i, \]

and model

\[ Z_i = \theta + e_i, \]

where \( (\theta) \) is the treatment effect and \( (e_i) \) are unobservable random variables.

Assumptions:

  • A1: \( (Z_i=\theta+e_i) \).
  • A2: \( (e_i) \) are mutually independent.
  • A3: Each \( (e_i) \) has a continuous distribution symmetric about 0 (not necessarily identical).

Procedure

Test

\[ H_0:\theta=0. \]

  1. Compute \( (|Z_i|) \), rank them: \( (R_i = \text{rank of } |Z_i|) \).
  2. Define \[ \psi_i = \begin{cases} 1 & Z_i>0 \\ 0 & Z_i<0 \end{cases} \]
  3. Define statistic \[ T^+ = \sum_{i=1}^n R_i \psi_i. \]

Interpretation: \( (T^+) \) is the sum of ranks corresponding to positive differences.

Define also

\[ T^- = \sum_{i=1}^n R_i(1-\psi_i), \quad T^+ + T^- = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}. \]

Null Distribution and Symmetry

Under \( (H_0) \), signs of \( (Z_i) \) are independent and equiprobable.

Thus each subset of positive ranks occurs with probability \( (2^{-n}) \), implying:

\[ T^+ \overset{d}{=} \sum_{i\in S} R_i, \quad S \subset \{1,\dots,n\}. \]

Hence distribution is distribution-free and depends only on ranks.

Symmetry Property

\[ P_0(T^+ \ge x) = P_0\left(T^+ \le \frac{n(n+1)}{2} - x \right). \]

Exact Tests

Let \( (t(\alpha,n)) \) satisfy:

\[ P_0(T^+ \ge t(\alpha,n)) = \alpha. \]

One-sided tests

  • \( (H_1:\theta>0) \) \[ \text{Reject if } T^+ \ge t(\alpha,n). \]
  • \( (H_1:\theta<0) \) \[ \text{Reject if } T^+ \le \frac{n(n+1)}{2} - t(\alpha,n). \]

Two-sided test

Reject if

\[ T^+ \ge t(\alpha_2,n) \quad \text{or} \quad T^+ \le \frac{n(n+1)}{2} - t(\alpha_1,n), \]

with \( (\alpha=\alpha_1+\alpha_2) \).

Large Sample Approximation

Under \( (H_0) \),

\[ \mathbb{E}(T^+) = \frac{n(n+1)}{4}, \quad \mathrm{Var}(T^+) = \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{24}. \]

Define standardized statistic:

\[ T^* = \frac{T^+ - \frac{n(n+1)}{4}}{\sqrt{\frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{24}}}. \]

Then

\[ T^* \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1). \]

Reject \( (H_0) \) if \( (T^* \ge z_\alpha) \).

Handling Ties

  • If some \( (Z_i=0) \): remove them and reduce \( (n) \).
  • If ties in \( (|Z_i|) \): use average ranks.

Variance correction:

\[ \mathrm{Var}_0(T^+) = \frac{1}{24}\left[n(n+1)(2n+1) - \frac{1}{2}\sum_{j=1}^g t_j(t_j-1)(t_j+1)\right], \]

where \( (t_j) \) are tie group sizes.

Distribution-Free Property

The null distribution depends only on symmetry and continuity, not on specific distributions.

Thus test controls:

\[ P(\text{Type I error}) = \alpha \]

uniformly over all distributions satisfying A1–A3.

Alternative Formulation

Define

\[ B = \#\{Z_i>0\}, \quad r_1<\cdots

Then

\[ T^+ = \sum_{i=1}^B r_i. \]

Each configuration occurs with probability \( (2^{-n}) \), yielding exact enumeration.

Generalization of Hypothesis

For testing \( (H_0:\theta=\theta_0) \), use:

\[ Z_i' = Z_i - \theta_0. \]

Comments and Interpretation

  • Test uses both signs and magnitudes (via ranks), unlike sign test.
  • Measures shift in location parameter.
  • Requires symmetry but not identical distributions.

Consistency

  • For \( (\theta>0) \): reject with probability \( (\to 1) \).
  • For \( (\theta<0) \): similarly.

Equivalent formulation:

\[ \theta^* = P(Z_1+Z_2>0) - \frac{1}{2}. \]

Consistency holds when \( (\theta^* \neq 0) \).

Efficiency

Wilcoxon signed rank test is asymptotically more efficient than the sign test under many distributions and is close to optimal under symmetric distributions.

Key Structural Insight

  • Statistic is a linear rank statistic.
  • Invariant under monotone transformations of \( (|Z_i|) \).
  • Distribution-free due to symmetry and random sign structure.

Distribution-Free Confidence Interval (Tukey–Wilcoxon Method)

Assume the paired-difference model \( (Z_i=\theta+e_i) \) with symmetry (A1–A3). A two-sided \( (1-\alpha) \) confidence interval is constructed using the Wilcoxon signed rank statistic \( (T^+) \).

Procedure

  1. Determine an integer \( (C_\alpha) \) such that \[ P_0\left(C_\alpha \le T^+ \le \frac{n(n+1)}{2}-C_\alpha\right)=1-\alpha. \]
  2. Form all pairwise averages: \[ W^{(1)} \le \cdots \le W^{(M)}, \quad W^{(k)}=\frac{Z_i+Z_j}{2}, \; i\le j,\; M=\frac{n(n+1)}{2}. \]
  3. Define endpoints: \[ \theta_L = W^{(C_\alpha)}, \quad \theta_U = W^{(M+1-C_\alpha)}. \]

Then

\[ P_\theta(\theta_L < \theta < \theta_U)=1-\alpha. \]

Justification

The interval corresponds to inversion of the two-sided Wilcoxon signed rank test: it contains all \( (\theta_0) \) not rejected by the level-\( (\alpha) \) test based on \( (Z_i-\theta_0) \). The coverage follows from the distribution-free property of \( (T^+) \) under symmetry.

Graphical construction (Tukey)

  • Plot ordered \( (Z_i) \).
  • Let \( (A=\min Z_i) \), \( (B=\max Z_i) \), and midpoint \( (C=(A+B)/2) \).
  • Construct triangle \( (ADB) \) and draw parallels through data points.
  • Intersections yield \( (W^{(C_\alpha)}) \) and \( (W^{(M+1-C_\alpha)}) \), corresponding to lower and upper bounds.

Large-sample approximation

\[ C_\alpha \approx \frac{n(n+1)}{4} - z_{\alpha/2}\sqrt{\frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{24}}, \]

rounded to nearest integer.

Interpretation and properties

  • The interval consists of all \( (\theta_0) \) accepted by the two-sided signed rank test.
  • The midpoint \[ \frac{W^{(C_\alpha)} + W^{(M+1-C_\alpha)}}{2} \] serves as a natural estimator of \( (\theta) \).
  • Under A1–A3, coverage probability is exactly \( (1-\alpha) \), independent of the underlying distributions (distribution-free).
  • Under stronger assumption (identically distributed errors), asymptotic standard error of the midpoint estimator can be consistently estimated via normal approximation.

Key characteristics

  • Nonparametric, distribution-free over symmetric continuous populations
  • Uses both signs and magnitudes (via ranks)
  • Robust to outliers relative to parametric intervals
  • Equivalent to inversion of Wilcoxon signed rank test
  • Efficient under symmetry assumptions
