Foundations of Data Depth and Multivariate Structure

Let \( (E) \) be a Banach space, \( (\mathcal{B}) \) its Borel \( (\sigma) \)-algebra, and \( (\mathcal{P}) \) a class of probability distributions on \( (\mathcal{B}) \), viewed as empirical distributions assigning equal mass \( (1/n) \) to \( (n) \) observations. A depth function is a mapping \( (D: E \times \mathcal{P} \to [0,1]) \), denoted \( (D(z\mid P)) \), or \( (D(z\mid X)) \) when \( (X\sim P) \), satisfying:

D1 Distribution invariance: \( (D(z+b\mid X+b)=D(z\mid X)) \) for all \( (b\in E) \).

D2 Linear invariance: \( (D(Az\mid AX)=D(z\mid X)) \) for every bijective linear transformation \( (A:E\to E) \).

D3 Null at infinity: \( (\lim_{|z|\to\infty} D(z\mid X)=0) \).

D4 Monotonicity on rays: if \( (z^*) \) maximizes depth, then for any unit vector \( (r) \), the function \( (\alpha \mapsto D(z^*+\alpha r\mid X)) \) is non-increasing (weak sense).

D5 Upper semicontinuity: upper level sets \( (D_\alpha(X)={z\in E: D(z\mid X)\ge \alpha}) \) are closed for all \( (\alpha) \).

D1 and D2 imply affine invariance. D3 and D4 ensure bounded, starshaped upper level sets with respect to a deepest point \( (z^*) \). D5 is a technical regularity condition. Under D4, if \( (X) \) is centrally symmetric about \( (z^*) \), then \( (D(z^*\mid X)) \) is maximal. Central symmetry means \( (X-z^*) \) and \( (z^*-X) \) have identical distributions. Compared to earlier formulations, these postulates do not require upper semicontinuity to ensure maximality at \( (z^*) \). Replacing D4 by D4con yields quasiconcavity, meaning all \( (D_\alpha(X)) \) are convex for \( (\alpha>0) \), defining convex depth. Convexity implies starshapedness and hence D4. D2 may be weakened to invariance under isometries (orthogonal invariance), or further to scale invariance \( (D(\lambda z\mid \lambda X)=D(z\mid X)) \) for \( (\lambda>0) \).

In practice, depth statistics are typically applied in Euclidean spaces \( (\mathbb{R}^d) \). For non-elliptical distributions, one considers distributions with affine dimension \( (d) \), i.e. convex hull of support equals \( (\mathbb{R}^d) \). A distribution is spherical if invariant under orthogonal transformations, and elliptical if \( (X=a+BY) \) with \( (Y) \) spherical. Elliptical distributions have upper level sets \( (D_\alpha(P)) \) forming ellipsoids determined by scatter matrix \( (BB') \). Any affine invariant depth satisfying D1 and D2 yields equivalent depth contours under ellipticity. If \( (P) \) is elliptical with unimodal radial density, there exists a transformation \( (\varphi) \) mapping it to a spherical distribution; thus any depth satisfying D1 and D2 is invariant under such transformations.

Three principal constructions of multivariate depth are distinguished: based on distances from central points or volumes; based on centrality notions such as decreasing weighted means of order statistics; and based on simplices and halfspaces in \( (\mathbb{R}^d) \). These approaches differ in symmetry reflection, robustness to outliers, and scalability in higher dimensions. Depth contours in applications often exhibit near-equidistant behavior across levels.

Most multivariate depths are convex and affine invariant; some also exhibit spherical invariance. Depths differ in contour shape and in whether they uniquely determine the underlying distribution. A fundamental ordering is induced via dilation order: \( (X \preceq_d Y) \) if \( (E[\varphi(X)] \le E[\varphi(Y)]) \) for all convex \( (\varphi:\mathbb{R}^d\to\mathbb{R}) \), raising the question of concordance between depth-based and dilation-based orderings.

Distance-Based Depth Functions

Let \( (E=\mathbb{R}^d) \). Depth based on distances measures outlyingness via distances from a chosen center.

\((L_2)\)-depth (spatial depth):

Defined by

\[ D^{L^2}(z\mid X)=\bigl(1+\mathbb{E}|z-X|\bigr)^{-1}. \]

For a sample \( (x^1,\dots,x^n) \),

\[ D^{L^2}(z\mid x^1,\dots,x^n)=\left(1+\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n |z-x^i|\right)^{-1}. \]

Range is \( ((0,1]) \) with maximum \( (1) \). Upper level sets are convex. The depth vanishes at infinity (D3) and is maximized at the spatial median \( (z^*=\arg\min_z \mathbb{E}|z-X|) \), which coincides with the symmetry center under central symmetry. It satisfies monotonicity on rays (D4), quasiconcavity (D4con), and upper semicontinuity (D5). It depends continuously on \( (z) \) and converges under mild integrability and weak convergence \( (P_n\to P) \). However, it is not consistent with the dilation order, since dilation increases \( (\mathbb{E}|z-X|) \), hence decreases depth.

It is invariant under rigid motions (D1, D2iso), but not affine invariant. An affine invariant version uses the \( (M) \)-norm \( (|z|*M=\sqrt{z'M^{-1}z}) \) with a positive definite matrix \( (M) \), typically \( (S_X) \) satisfying affine equivariance \( (S*{AX+b}=AS_XA') \):

\[ D(z\mid X)=\bigl(1+\mathbb{E}|z-X|_{S_X}\bigr)^{-1}. \]

Sample versions arise by replacing expectations with empirical averages. Common choices for \( (S_X) \) include the covariance matrix or robust estimators such as MVE or MCD.

Mahalanobis depth:

Let \( (c_X) \) be a location functional and \( (S_X) \) a positive definite scatter matrix satisfying affine equivariance. The generalized Mahalanobis depth is

\[ D^{\text{Mah}}(z\mid X)=\left(1+|z-c_X|*{S_X}^2\right)^{-1}. \]

It satisfies D1 to D5. Upper level sets are ellipsoids

\[ D*\alpha^{\text{Mah}}(X)={z:|z-c_X|_{S_X}^2\le \alpha^{-1}-1}, \]

centered at \( (c_X) \). It is continuous in \( (z) \) and in \( (P) \). With \( (c_X=\mathbb{E}[X]) \), \( (S_X=\Sigma_X) \),

\[ D^{\text{Mah}}(z\mid X)=\left(1+(z-\mathbb{E}[X])'\Sigma_X^{-1}(z-\mathbb{E}[X])\right)^{-1}, \]

and its sample version replaces parameters by empirical estimates. Depth contours depend strongly on the choice of \( (S_X) \) (classical vs robust). Mahalanobis depth is determined by first two moments; it does not uniquely identify distributions beyond elliptical families, though within such families contour sets suffice.

Other depths (formulas and notation only):

Projection depth:

\[ D^{\text{proj}}(z\mid X)=\left(1+\sup_{p\in S^{d-1}} \frac{|\langle p,z\rangle-\operatorname{med}(\langle p,X\rangle)|}{\operatorname{Dmed}(\langle p,X\rangle)}\right)^{-1}, \]

where \( (S^{d-1}) \) is the unit sphere, \( (\langle p,X\rangle) \) denotes projection, \( (\operatorname{med}) \) is the univariate median, and \( (\operatorname{Dmed}) \) is the median absolute deviation.

Oja depth:

\[ D^{\text{Oja}}(z\mid X)=\left(1+\frac{\mathbb{E}\big[\operatorname{vol}_d(\operatorname{co}{z,X_1,\dots,X_d})\big]}{\sqrt{\det \Sigma_X}}\right)^{-1}, \]

where \( (X_1,\dots,X_d) \) are i.i.d. copies of \( (X) \), \( (\operatorname{co}) \) denotes convex hull, \( (\operatorname{vol}_d) \) the \( (d) \)-dimensional volume, and \( (\Sigma_X) \) a scatter matrix.

Depth-Trimmed Regions and Outlyingness

For a probability distribution \( (P) \) and level \( (\alpha \in [0,1]) \), the upper level sets

\[ D_\alpha(P)={z\in E: D(z\mid P)\ge \alpha} \]

form a nested family of sets (larger \( (\alpha) \) gives smaller sets). These are called depth-trimmed central regions.

  • The deepest points are those achieving maximal depth: \[ P_{\max}(P)={z: D(z\mid P)=\sup_{x} D(x\mid P)}. \]
  • By D1 and D2, these regions are affine equivariant (stable under translation and linear transformations).
  • Their shape depends on the underlying distribution \( (P) \) (location, dispersion, geometry).

There is a correspondence with convex set-valued statistics:

Given a family \( ({C_\alpha(P)}*{\alpha\in[0,1]}) \) of convex, closed, bounded sets, define

\[ D(z\mid P)=\sup{\alpha: z\in C*\alpha(P)}. \]

This construction satisfies D1–D5 and D4con, hence yields a convex depth function. Thus, depth functions and central regions are equivalent representations.

A depth function induces a center-outward ordering of points: higher depth implies more central.

Define the associated outlyingness function:

\[ \text{Out}(z\mid X)=\frac{1}{D(z\mid X)}-1. \]

Key properties:

  • \( (\text{Out}(z\mid X)=0) \) at the center (maximum depth).
  • \( (\text{Out}(z\mid X)\to \infty) \) as \( (|z|\to\infty) \) (since depth \( (\to 0) \)).

Equivalently,

\[ D(z\mid X) = \bigl(1+\text{Out}(z\mid X)\bigr)^{-1}. \]

Interpretation:

  • Points outside the central region \( (D_\alpha) \) satisfy \[ D(z\mid X)<\alpha \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \text{Out}(z\mid X) > \frac{1}{\alpha}-1, \] hence are classified as outliers at level \( (\alpha) \).

Limitations of Spatial Depth and Motivation for Kernelization

A contour plot of the spatial depth \( D(x \mid X) \) illustrates that depth is highest at the center (spatial median) and decreases outward. Thus, low depth corresponds to peripheral or isolated points, motivating the outlier rule:

\( \text{Classify } z \text{ as an outlier if } D(z \mid X) < t \)

for a suitable threshold \( t \in (0,1) \).

Two methodological issues arise:

  • Selection of the threshold.
  • Whether spatial depth reflects the geometric structure of the data cloud.

Behavior under spherical symmetry

For spherically symmetric distributions, depth contours align with the data geometry (approximately concentric and circular), so depth is inversely related to distance from the center. Hence, low depth reliably indicates outlyingness.

Failure under non-elliptical structure

However, for non-elliptical or complex shapes, this relationship breaks down. The failure arises from the definition:

\( D(x \mid X) = 1 - \left\| \mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{x - X}{\|x - X\|} \right] \right\| \)

  • Points located centrally in terms of geometry may have low depth.
  • Points in low-density regions may still have high depth due to directional cancellation.

Thus, spatial depth does not necessarily align with Euclidean distance or probability density.

Geometric inconsistency

  • Depth contours do not follow the intrinsic shape of the data.
  • Central regions may exclude high-density areas.
  • The induced ordering may not reflect true centrality.

Comparison with Mahalanobis depth

\( D^{\mathrm{Mah}}(z \mid X) = \left(1 + (z - \mu)' \Sigma^{-1} (z - \mu) \right)^{-1} \)

  • Produces elliptical contours regardless of data shape.
  • Fails for non-elliptical distributions.
  • Larger values indicate centrality, unlike spatial depth where smaller values indicate outlyingness.

Fundamental trade-off

Spatial depth depends on the sum of unit direction vectors:

  • Emphasizes direction over magnitude
  • Provides robustness to extreme observations
  • Reduces sensitivity to distance-based separation

Thus,

Robustness to extremes \( \Longleftrightarrow \) Loss of geometric sensitivity

Motivation for kernelization

To address these limitations, spatial depth can be modified by incorporating a similarity measure via a positive definite kernel. Let \( \phi \) be the associated feature map.

\( D(x \mid X) = 1 - \left\| \mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{\phi(x) - \phi(X)}{\|\phi(x) - \phi(X)\|} \right] \right\| \)

  • Captures nonlinear structure
  • Incorporates similarity instead of raw distance
  • Retains robustness through normalization

Kernelized Spatial Depth

A positive definite kernel \( (\kappa:\mathbb{R}^n\times \mathbb{R}^n\to \mathbb{R}) \) induces an implicit feature mapping \( (\phi:\mathbb{R}^n\to \mathbb{F}) \) into an inner product space \( (\mathbb{F}) \) such that

\[ \kappa(x,y)=\langle \phi(x),\phi(y)\rangle. \]

For stationary kernels (e.g. Gaussian \( (\kappa(x,y)=\exp(-|x-y|^2/\sigma^2)) \)), \( (\kappa(x,y)) \) acts as a similarity measure.

The kernelized spatial depth (KSD) evaluates spatial depth in the feature space \( (\mathbb{F}) \). Using the identity

\[ |x-y|^2 = x^Tx + y^Ty - 2x^Ty, \]

the spatial depth expression can be rewritten in terms of inner products and then replaced by kernel evaluations. This yields the sample KSD:

\[ D_\kappa(x\mid X) = 1-\frac{1}{|\mathcal{X}(x)|-1} \left( \sum_{y,z\in \mathcal{X}(x)} \frac{\kappa(x,x)+\kappa(y,z)-\kappa(x,y)-\kappa(x,z)} {\delta_\kappa(x,y)\,\delta_\kappa(x,z)} \right)^{1/2}, \]

where

\[ \delta_\kappa(x,y)=\sqrt{\kappa(x,x)+\kappa(y,y)-2\kappa(x,y)}, \]

and terms with \( (x=y) \) or \( (x=z) \) are set to zero (analogous to the spatial sign at zero).

KSD is a spatial depth in the feature space \( (\mathbb{F}) \), but not necessarily a depth in \( (\mathbb{R}^n) \) since the feature-space center may not correspond to any point in the original space. A new “center” in \( (\mathbb{R}^n) \) is defined as the point maximizing KSD; however, monotonicity away from this center is not guaranteed.

The formulation is valid for any positive definite kernel, with emphasis on stationary kernels. Empirically, KSD contours adapt to the intrinsic geometry of the data, unlike standard spatial depth:

  • For non-elliptical distributions (e.g. half-moon, ring-shaped), KSD contours follow the data shape.
  • Depth values at isolated points are small, enabling effective outlier detection.

Example values: depths at candidate outliers are approximately \( (0.2495) \) (half-moon) and \( (0.2651) \) (ring). A threshold around \( (0.25)–(0.27) \) separates outliers from the main data cloud. Determination of the threshold is addressed separately.
