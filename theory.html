Outlier Detection Problem

The problem of outlier detection can be stated as follows: given observations \( X_1, \dots, X_n \in \mathbb{R}^d \) drawn from an unknown distribution \( P \), possibly contaminated by a small fraction of observations from another distribution \( Q \), identify the subset of indices corresponding to observations that do not conform to \( P \).

Formally, under a contamination model:

\( X_i \sim (1 - \varepsilon)P + \varepsilon Q, \quad 0 < \varepsilon \ll 1 \)

the task is to decide, based only on the sample, which \( X_i \)'s are generated from \( Q \). The difficulty lies in the absence of a canonical definition of extremeness, especially in higher dimensions where geometric intuition and distance-based separation become less reliable.

The intuitive need for a depth function arises precisely from this lack of ordering in \( \mathbb{R}^d \). In one dimension, outliers can be identified using the natural ordering of the real line, but in higher dimensions no such total order exists.

A depth function provides a center-outward ranking of points, assigning higher values to central observations and lower values to peripheral ones. This allows us to define outliers as points with sufficiently small depth, thereby giving a principled, geometry-aware notion of extremeness that generalizes univariate ideas like medians, quantiles, and tail behavior.

Data Depth

A depth function assigns a notion of centrality to points with respect to a probability distribution \( P \). Formally, a depth function is a map

\( D : \mathbb{R}^d \times \mathcal{P}(\mathbb{R}^d) \to [0,1] \),

where larger values indicate greater centrality and smaller values indicate outlyingness.

Axioms of Depth Functions

A well-defined depth function typically satisfies the following axioms:

  1. Affine invariance: For any nonsingular affine transformation \( T(x) = Ax + b \), \[ D(x;P) = D(Tx; P_T), \] where \( P_T \) is the pushforward of \( P \).
  2. Maximality at center: If \( P \) has a center of symmetry \( \theta \), then \[ D(\theta;P) = \sup_x D(x;P). \]
  3. Monotonicity relative to deepest point: Along any ray from a deepest point \( \theta \), depth decreases monotonically.
  4. Vanishing at infinity: \[ D(x;P) \to 0 \quad \text{as } \|x\| \to \infty. \]

Distance-Based Depth

Distance-based depth functions quantify the centrality of a point \( x \in \mathbb{R}^d \) by measuring its distance from a representative location of the distribution.

A general form is:

\( D(x;P) = g\big(d(x, \theta(P))\big), \)

where \( d(\cdot,\cdot) \) is a metric, \( \theta(P) \) is a location functional, and \( g \) is a decreasing function.

Examples include Mahalanobis depth, \( L_2 \) depth, and Oja depth.

The Mahalanobis depth is defined as:

\( D(x;P) = \left[ 1 + (x - \mu)^\top \Sigma^{-1} (x - \mu) \right]^{-1}. \)

While affine invariant, Mahalanobis depth is sensitive to outliers unless robust estimators of \( \mu \) and \( \Sigma \) are used.

Kernel-Based Depth

Kernel-based depth functions can be defined at the population level by replacing empirical averages with expectations.

For a kernel \( K : \mathbb{R}^d \times \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R} \),

\( D(x;P) = \mathbb{E}_{X \sim P}[K(x,X)] = \int_{\mathbb{R}^d} K(x,y)\, dP(y). \)

A kernel \( K \) is positive definite if for any \( n \in \mathbb{N} \), points \( x_1,\dots,x_n \), and coefficients \( c_1,\dots,c_n \),

\( \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n c_i c_j K(x_i,x_j) \ge 0. \)

This formulation measures centrality via similarity to the distribution.

Kernelized Spatial Depth (KSD)

Kernelized spatial depth extends classical spatial depth by incorporating a positive definite kernel.

Let \( \phi : \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathcal{F} \) be a feature map such that \( K(x,y) = \langle \phi(x), \phi(y) \rangle \).

The population definition is:

\( D(x;P) = 1 - \mathbb{E}_{X \sim P} \left\| \frac{\phi(x) - \phi(X)}{\|\phi(x) - \phi(X)\|} \right\|. \)

The sample version is:

\( D(x;P_n) = 1 - \frac{1}{n+1} \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\phi(x) - \phi(X_i)}{\|\phi(x) - \phi(X_i)\|}. \)

Using the identity \( \|\phi(x)-\phi(X_i)\|^2 = K(x,x) + K(X_i,X_i) - 2K(x,X_i) \), the expression can be computed entirely in terms of kernel values.

A common choice is the Gaussian kernel:

\( K(x,y) = \exp\left(-\frac{\|x-y\|^2}{2\sigma^2}\right), \)

where \( \sigma > 0 \) is the bandwidth parameter.
