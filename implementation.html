From Theory to Real-World Data

Applying Kernelized Spatial Depth to NHANES healthcare datasets to detect hidden metabolic risk.

Dataset

We use data from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES), covering three cycles:

  • 2015–2016 (n = 626)
  • 2017–2018 (n = 639)
  • 2021–2023 (n = 849)

Analysis is restricted to individuals aged ≥ 18 with normal BMI (18.5–25), focusing on detecting hidden metabolic abnormalities.

Key variables: fasting glucose, HbA1c, triglycerides, HDL cholesterol, age.

Preprocessing

  • Complete case filtering (remove missing values)
  • Restriction to normal BMI range
  • Z-score standardization of all variables

Standardization ensures that no variable dominates due to scale differences.

Outlier Detection Methods

Mahalanobis Distance (MD)

  • Compute distance from mean using covariance structure
  • Outliers = top 5% of distances (95th percentile)

Kernelized Spatial Depth (KSD)

  • Gaussian kernel used
  • Bandwidth σ chosen via median distance
  • Outliers = bottom 5% of depth values
KSD captures nonlinear structure, while MD assumes elliptical geometry.

Statistical Analysis

We compare observations uniquely identified by each method: KSD-only vs MD-only.

A one-sided Wilcoxon rank-sum test is used to evaluate:

\( H_0: HbA1c_{KSD} = HbA1c_{MD} \quad \text{vs} \quad H_1: HbA1c_{KSD} > HbA1c_{MD} \)

Purpose: Determine whether KSD identifies clinically more extreme cases.

Results

  • Across all datasets, KSD-only outliers show higher HbA1c levels
  • Statistically significant differences (p < 0.05 in all cases)
  • KSD identifies clinically meaningful deviations missed by MD
Conclusion: Kernelized Spatial Depth provides superior detection of hidden metabolic risk compared to Mahalanobis distance.
